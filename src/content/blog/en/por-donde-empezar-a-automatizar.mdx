Most automation projects fail for the same reason: they start with the tool. Someone buys software, signs up for a subscription and, weeks later, it's still there, half-configured, while the team works exactly as before. Good automation starts earlier, and somewhere else: by looking at where time goes.

## Start with the pain, not the tool

Before thinking about which technology to use, identify **which process is costing you money today**. Not the flashiest or the most modern one: the one that eats the most hours, generates the most errors, or blocks the rest of the team whenever someone is on holiday.

That process is your starting point. The tool comes later, as a consequence of the problem — not the other way around.

## Look for repetitive tasks with clear rules

Not everything automates equally well. The best candidates share three traits:

- **They repeat** often (daily or weekly), not once a quarter.
- **They follow clear rules**: "if this happens, do that," without too many exceptions.
- **They move data between places**: from an email to a spreadsheet, from a form to your CRM, from an invoice to your accounting.

If a task ticks all three, there's almost always room to take it off a person's plate.

## Measure before touching anything

Before changing anything, put a number on the table. How many hours a week does the team spend on that task? How much does that hour cost? Without that figure you won't know whether automation is worth it, nor will you be able to prove the result afterwards.

If you want a quick estimate, our [automation savings calculator](/recursos/calculadora-roi) gives you a ballpark figure in a minute.

## Automate a whole process, not half a task

A common mistake is to automate only the easy part and leave the rest by hand. The trouble is that the "rest by hand" forces someone to stay on top of it, and the real saving disappears.

It's better to take **one small process and run it end to end** —so it works on its own, with no one watching over it— than to half-touch ten processes.

> The best automation is the one you don't notice: the work simply stops showing up on your team's to-do list.

## A realistic first step

You don't need to transform the company in a month. Start with one process, measure it, automate it end to end and check the result. With that first win —time recovered and errors gone— the next step decides itself.
